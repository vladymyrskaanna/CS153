# PROMPT 03 — Write the Cold Email

Use this prompt after you have a completed dossier. Paste the full dossier into Claude along with this prompt.

---

## The Prompt

```
Based on the attached dossier, please produce a cold email for outreach to [TARGET PERSON NAME], [TITLE] at [COMPANY].

The email should follow this exact 6-paragraph structure:

## Paragraph 1 — The hook
Open with a vivid, specific founding moment from the family history. Name the founder and the first pivotal deal. Use the construction "a single decision that would define the next [N] years" to compress the pivotal moment. End with "He built the company through [the major historical events of his era]."

The founding moment MUST be:
- Specific (not "your grandfather founded the company in 1950")
- Vivid (a phone call, a handshake, a war story, a radio ad)
- Sourced (every claim must trace back to a URL in the dossier)

## Paragraph 2 — Generational bridge
A single sentence: "Each generation after him faced its own defining test and took the business to a higher level."

Optionally extend with one more sentence naming the recipient's own defining moment, if it's public and non-controversial. For example, for Sarah Matesich Schwab: "You are now doing the same — not only leading Matesich, but breaking new ground as the first woman to chair the Anheuser-Busch Wholesaler Advisory Panel."

## Paragraph 3 — The turning point
Exactly these three sentences, slightly rephrased if needed:
"We are now entering another historic turning point: the AI revolution. Like the Industrial Revolution, it will fundamentally reshape the economy and the way business is done — and this is a rare opportunity for leaders like you to take the business to its next level."

## Paragraph 4 — My personal story
"My name is Anna. Like you, I grew up in the beer and beverage distribution business. As a kid, I took orders, helped restock shelves, and fixed trucks with my dad. That is why I decided to build AI Intelligence — to help family businesses like the one I grew up in thrive in the age of AI."

## Paragraph 5 — Credibility (AI Intelligence)
"Today, AI Intelligence works with leading distributors such as Manhattan Beer, and our technology is already used in more than 20,000 stores across the U.S. AI Intelligence was built at Stanford and recognized by leading programs including Google for Startups, Techstars by JPMorgan Chase, NVIDIA, Stanford Emergence Accelerator, and Stanford Graduate School of Business Demo Day. From day one, we have been focused on building AI specifically for the real needs of the CPG industry. Our technology can equip [Company] to reach new heights in this unique moment."

## Paragraph 6 — The ask
"My team and I will be in Washington for the NBWA Legislative Conference starting this Sunday. It would be great to grab a coffee and discuss how [Company] can lead the next wave of AI transformation in our industry."

## Sign-off
Cheers,
Anna
Mobile:  · Web: [WEB URL]

---

## Constraints

- Total length: 200-400 words
- Use em-dashes (—) not hyphens (-)
- No bullet points, no lists, all prose
- No buzzwords ("synergy", "leverage", "disrupt", "transformational")
- The word "AI" appears only in paragraphs 3 and 5-6 — nowhere else
- Don't over-personalize: 2-3 specific facts about the recipient is correct, 7 is creepy
- Don't mention anything potentially sensitive: lawsuits, recent deaths, EEOC filings, family disputes

## Subject line

Propose 3 subject line options:
1. A "What Would [Ancestor] Do in the Age of AI?" style clickbait (if family legacy is clean)
2. A direct business ask referencing NBWA Conference (always safe)
3. A personal, name-in-subject option (good for high-priority targets)

## Critical red flags check

Before writing, scan the dossier for these:
- Is there an active lawsuit? → Default to generic "your family has built a multi-generational legacy" opener. Don't name anyone in the first paragraph.
- Is there a recent death (< 12 months)? → Add a brief condolence line before the pitch. Consider delaying outreach.
- Is there an EEOC history? → Don't mention anything related to the company's employment practices.
- Is the target a woman? → Consider referencing any "first woman to" achievements publicly associated with her in paragraph 2.

If any of these apply, output BOTH a "standard" version and a "safe" version, clearly labeled, and explain the choice.
```

## Common iteration patterns

These are the most common rewrite requests from real users; be ready for them.

### "Make it shorter"
Strip concrete dates and ages. Keep the vivid moment. Compress paragraphs 4-5 into one.

### "Too many numbers"
Remove years (1928, age 17, 90 years, etc.) except the anchor phrase "the next ninety years." Let the cinema of the moment carry the weight.

### "Wrong generation"
Re-check the family tree. Great-grandfather vs. grandfather depends on who you're writing to. For 3rd-gen target: founder = grandfather. For 4th-gen target: founder = great-grandfather. Be careful.

### "Don't name anyone"
Use "Your family has built a remarkable multi-generational legacy in this industry. Founded [N] years ago, the company has moved beer through the Great Depression, World War II, and every wave of industry change since."

### "Add women-in-industry context"
For Sarah Matesich Schwab (Matesich): "first female chair of the Anheuser-Busch Wholesaler Advisory Panel and first woman elected to the WBWAO board." Use the word "elected" rather than just "on" to avoid claiming too much.

### "Make it more aggressive / more AI-focused"
Keep the legacy frame in paragraph 1 — that's what gets you past the spam filter and into the executive's attention. Push AI emphasis in paragraphs 5-6, never in paragraph 1.

## Examples of strong opening paragraphs (by target)

**Sarah Matesich Schwab (Matesich):**
> "Your great-grandfather John founded the company after hearing an ad for Prima Near Beer on the radio during a baseball game — he picked up the phone, called the brewery, and asked for a distributorship. A few years later, he bought the Budweiser rights from a failing competitor — a single decision that would define the next ninety years. He built the company through Prohibition, the Great Depression, and World War II."

**Harry Kampakis (AlaBev):**
> "Your father Sergei came home from the Navy in 1945 and, twelve years later, bought a Birmingham beer distributor. He built it through the Schlitz collapse, the craft-beer revolution, and the big brewer consolidations — a hundred-year name you and your son Sergei carry today."

**Nick Amendola (Valley Wide Beverage):**
> "Your father Lou once described the scale of what he built with three simple words: 'Not even a little bit.' That was him answering whether he ever imagined, back in 1981 when he bought one small distributorship in Yuba City at thirty-one, that Valley Wide would become what it is now."

## Examples of when to break the template

**Ed Kozol (Kozol Bros):**
Active lawsuit in the family. Generic opener only:
> "Your family has built a remarkable multi-generational legacy in this industry. Founded just one year after the repeal of Prohibition, the company has been moving beer out of Joliet for more than ninety years — through the Great Depression, World War II, and every wave of industry change since."

**Ryan O'Connor (O'Connor Distributing):**
Chairman died April 1, 2026. Condolence line + pitch delayed:
> "First — my condolences on George's passing. The O'Connor legacy in Arkansas beer is exactly that — a legacy — and I know the last several weeks have been a profound weight for the whole family and the business..."

Then wait 30-60 days before sending the main pitch.
